## The Eight Proposed Veins — Ranked by Priority

### 🥇 VEIN 1 — MONUMENTS, ENGINEERING & ASTRONOMY *(Top Priority)*

**Rationale:** India's built heritage is among the most spectacular on earth, and each monument hides an engineering story that is inseparable from its historical context — who commissioned it, who carved it, what astronomical or hydraulic principle it embodies. These are zero-biography-required episodes: the *building* is the hero, and child-sized wonder ("how did they do that?") is the hook. Every monument below has UNESCO World Heritage status plus ASI excavation reports and epigraphic records — meeting the highest sourcing standard. Visually, towering stone chariots, underground stepwells, and rock-hewn temples are shadow-puppet gold.

#### Candidate Episode Titles

1. **"The Sun's Shadow at Konark"** — The Konark Sun Temple (13th c.) as a giant stone chariot with 24 carved wheels that function as sundials, built under King Narasimhadeva I of the Eastern Ganga dynasty; focuses on the artisans and the chariot-temple concept.
2. **"One Mountain, One Temple: Kailasa at Ellora"** — The Kailasa Temple (c. 756-773 CE), carved top-to-bottom from a single basalt cliff by Rashtrakuta king Krishna I; the episode is about carving *away* rather than *building up* — uniquely dramatic.
3. **"Sanchi's Four Gates Tell Stories in Stone"** — The torana gateways of the Great Stupa (Sunga/Satavahana period, 1st c. BCE–CE), with donor inscriptions naming ordinary monks, merchants, ivory-workers, and women, showing that great monuments are community projects.
4. **"Rani-ki-Vav: The Queen's Stairway to Water"** — The Patan stepwell (c. 1063 CE), commissioned by Queen Udayamati as a memorial and water system; seven levels of carved galleries descending to the water; a story about engineering + grief + patronage.
5. **"Hampi's Bazaar of a Million Things"** — The 1-km straight market street of Vijayanagara, documented by eyewitness Domingo Paes; daily life in the world's second-largest city of the 16th century.
6. **"The Pillar That Would Not Rust"** — The Mehrauli Iron Pillar (4th-5th c. CE); its 1,600-year resistance to rust as evidence of ancient Indian metallurgy (high-phosphorus iron and phosphate passivation layer); the inscription of King Chandra as a detective puzzle.
7. **"Dholavira's Stone Channel and Sixteen Reservoirs"** — The Harappan city of Dholavira (c. 3000-1500 BCE) and its unprecedented water-harvesting network in an island with no river — a feat of hydrological planning linking to Vein 8 (Harappan civilization).
8. **"The Builders of Brihadisvara's Vimana"** — A companion episode to the existing "Rajaraja Raises Brihadisvara" focused on the *artisans and sthapatis* (master builders) named in temple inscriptions; the engineering question of the 216-ft vimana capstone.

#### Sensitivity Notes
- **Konark:** The "12 years, 1,200 artisans" legend is noted in UNESCO dossier but its source is the Madala Panji (a late chronicle) — label as tradition. The astronomical sundial interpretation of the wheels is credible and documented by S. Balachandra Rao (*Indian Journal of History of Science* 37, 2002) but present as an "interpretation" not proven mechanical fact.
- **Kailasa:** No battle imagery needed; the episode is about carving downward, which is visually unique and epistemically uncontested.
- **Iron Pillar:** "King Chandra" identification with Chandragupta II is the scholarly consensus but not unanimous (D.C. Sircar's caution should be noted); the metallurgy is independently verified.
- **No communal sensitivity** — monuments span Buddhist (Sanchi), Hindu (Konark, Brihadisvara, Kailasa), and functional/civic (Rani-ki-Vav, Dholavira) traditions, modelling inclusive Indian heritage.

### 🥈 VEIN 2 — SCIENCE, MATHEMATICS & MEDICINE *(High Priority — extends proven Aryabhata/Brahmagupta format)*

**Rationale:** The single existing episode (Aryabhata + Brahmagupta combined) proves the format works. India's contributions to surgery, mathematics, pharmacy, and astronomy are internationally recognised, primary-text-grounded, and celebrate intellectual achievement in a way that resonates with families. Extending to Sushruta, Charaka, Bhaskara II, Madhava, and Varahamihira covers 1,500 years of scholarly tradition across multiple regions. Every figure below has a surviving primary text as the source anchor.

#### Candidate Episode Titles

1. **"Sushruta's Careful Hands"** — Surgery documented in the *Sushruta Samhita*: 120 surgical instruments, rhinoplasty, cataract surgery; told as a student entering the Kashi school of Divodasa. Age-band B/C.
2. **"Charaka Asks Seventy Questions"** — The *Charaka Samhita*'s systematic diagnosis approach: listening to the patient, the three-dosha framework, the idea that a physician must observe before prescribing. The episode centers on the physician asking rather than knowing.
3. **"Varahamihira's Book of Everything"** — The *Brihat Samhita* (6th c. CE) as an encyclopaedia covering comets, weather, gemstones, perfumes, earthquakes, and architecture — a child's tour through the world according to a great mind.
4. **"Bhaskara and the Clever Problems"** — Bhaskara II's *Lilavati* (1150 CE): mathematical puzzles about swans, lotuses, and honey-bees framing algebraic problems; the episode is structured as a puzzle-game.
5. **"Madhava Counts to Infinity"** — Madhava of Sangamagrama (Kerala, c. 1340-1425 CE) and his infinite series for pi and the sine function, centuries before Europe; grounded in the *Yuktibhasha* commentary. Celebrates regional (Kerala) genius.
6. **"The Pharmacy of the Forest"** — Charaka/Vagbhata on medicinal plants (*dravyaguna*); connects science to ecology; uses the *Ashtangahridaya* of Vagbhata (7th c. CE) as primary text.

#### Sensitivity Notes
- **Bhaskara's "daughter Lilavati":** The framing story that Bhaskara wrote the text for his daughter whose horoscope predicted widowhood is a charming literary tradition, not a documented biographical fact. Use the puzzles from the *Lilavati* directly; label the daughter-story as "tradition says."
- **Madhava's priority:** The claim that the Kerala school developed infinite series before Leibniz/Gregory/Newton is supported by Plofker, Joseph, and Sarma and is not contested in serious mathematical history. It can be stated as scholarly consensus, not as Indian triumphalism.
- **Surgery details:** Rhinoplasty and cataract surgery episodes must be written with care for age-band C only; describe instruments and technique in wonder-of-skill terms, not graphic clinical detail.

### 🥉 VEIN 3 — WOMEN RULERS & LEADERS ACROSS ERAS *(High Priority — dedicated new section)*

**Rationale:** The existing collection has women primarily as fighters or martyrs (1857, Quit India). A dedicated "Women Who Ruled" section would celebrate *executive authority* — queens who issued inscriptions, administered territories, commissioned monuments, and debated in courts. These are not legends: they are documented by copper-plate grants, epigraphy, coinage, and contemporary chronicles. This fills a real structural gap and expands regional coverage (Andhra, Kashmir, Gujarat, Deccan, Maratha).

#### Candidate Episode Titles

1. **"Rudrama Devi's Golden Coins"** — Kakatiya queen (~1262-1289 CE) who ruled under a male royal name/titles on coinage and inscriptions, mentioned by Marco Polo as a "queen of Telingana"; demonstrates documented gender-transgressive rulership.
2. **"Ahilyabai's Lamp Burns All Night"** — Holkar queen of Malwa (r. 1767-1795): administrative reforms, temple construction across India, management of the Narmada ghats, documented in Marathi records and John Malcolm's *Memoir of Central India* (1823). Age-band B.
3. **"Didda of Kashmir: The Grandmother Regent"** — Kalhana's *Rajatarangini* (Book VI) describes Didda ruling Kashmir (~979-1003 CE) first as regent then as queen regnant; the episode follows one specific inscriptionally-supported policy she enacted.
4. **"Naganika's Gift at the Cave"** — The Nanaghat Rock Inscription (~1st c. BCE), Satavahana queen Naganika's own record of sacrificial gifts; possibly the earliest epigraphic record of an Indian queen in her own voice. Short, age-band B.
5. **"Prabhavatigupta's Copper Plate"** — Vakataka queen and daughter of Chandragupta II (~385-405 CE), who issued copper-plate land grants in her own name while ruling as regent; demonstrates how women wielded administrative power even in patrilineal dynasties.
6. **"Rani Durgavati Keeps the Gondwana"** — Gond queen (d. 1564 CE), who ruled Gondwana for 15+ years and fell facing Asaf Khan's imperial campaign; documented in Abul Fazl's *Akbarnama*.
7. **"Trailokyanatha's Queen Builds the Stepwell"** — Queen Udayamati of the Chaulukya dynasty commissions Rani-ki-Vav (c. 1063 CE) as a water system and memorial; links to Vein 1 Monuments.

#### Sensitivity Notes
- **Rudrama Devi:** Her use of male titulature is a documented fact, not a modern feminist imposition. Episode should note this without either sensationalising or erasing it.
- **Ahilyabai:** Substantial hagiographical literature exists alongside genuine historical record. Ground claims in Malcolm (a contemporary observer), avoid the miraculous-devotion layer.
- **Didda:** *Rajatarangini* depicts her as both powerful and ruthless (accused of poisoning rivals). For a child-facing episode, focus on her governance policies and the documented fact of female rule, without the palace intrigue.
- **Communal neutrality:** Rulers span Hindu, Jain (Naganika's Satavahana context), and composite traditions; no single community framing.

### 4 — HARAPPAN & INDUS CIVILISATION *(Strong Priority — visually unique, communally neutral)*

**Rationale:** The Harappan civilization (c. 3000-1300 BCE) is one of the world's largest ancient urban cultures, yet is almost entirely absent from Indian children's content. It is *epistemically safe* — the script is undeciphered, no texts exist, so there are no religious claims, no dynastic heroes, no communal ownership disputes. Every episode is grounded purely in ASI archaeological reports. The visual world — terracotta figurines, standard weights, drainage systems, unicorn seals, beadmakers' workshops — is extraordinary for illustrated or shadow-puppet storytelling.

#### Candidate Episode Titles

1. **"Dholavira's Sixteen Reservoirs"** — The water-harvesting city on Khadir island: 16 reservoirs, stone-cut channels, and a sophisticated run-off capture system for a city with no river; based on R.S. Bisht's excavation report.
2. **"The Dock at Lothal"** — The Lothal dockyard (~2400 BCE): the world's earliest known tidal dock, with a sluice gate allowing ships to enter at high tide; based on S.R. Rao's ASI excavation report.
3. **"The Same Weight Everywhere"** — Standardised chert cube weights found from Harappa to Mohenjo-daro to Lothal to Dholavira; the episode explores what standard weights imply about trade, governance, and shared norms.
4. **"What the Seals Say"** — The undeciphered Indus script and unicorn/zebu seals; told as a mystery episode where the children are the investigators; celebrates the fact that some history is still being discovered.
5. **"The Bead Road from Lothal to Sumer"** — Carnelian beads from Lothal found at Mesopotamian sites; the episode reconstructs the Indian Ocean trade in micro-detail using archaeological provenance evidence.
6. **"Rakhigarhi: The Giant City Rediscovered"** — Recent ASI/Deccan College excavations revealing Rakhigarhi (Haryana) as the largest Harappan site yet found; the episode centers on the act of archaeological discovery itself.

#### Sensitivity Notes
- **Critical caution — ethnic/linguistic identity:** Do NOT engage with the "Aryan Migration Theory," Proto-Dravidian language claims, or any connection to present-day communities. The Harappan script is undeciphered and no scholarly consensus exists on the language(s) spoken. Every episode must stay strictly within what physical archaeology tells us. The roadmap's "evidence over legend" standard applies with particular force here.
- **No religious framing:** The "priest-king" figurine and "proto-Shiva" seal interpretations are speculative. Present them as "a carved figure scholars have named..." not as religious origin stories.
- **Age-band note:** Harappan episodes work best for Age Band B/C (6-12); the mystery-of-discovery framing is highly engaging.
- **Positive framing:** The episodes celebrate ingenuity, planning, trade, and civic organisation — entirely child-safe and uplifting.

### 5 — SPIRITUAL & PHILOSOPHICAL FIGURES AS HISTORY *(Strong Priority — multi-faith; requires most careful source-handling)*

**Rationale:** India's tradition of teacher-founders (Buddha, Mahavira, Shankara, Basavanna, Kabir, Guru Nanak, Andal, Meerabai) is globally renowned but largely told through hagiography. A "told as history, not myth" approach — focusing on what documentary evidence confirms about their lives and *actual texts they wrote or are attributed with* — would make these episodes unique among children's content. The historical footprint is real: Ashokan edicts confirm the Buddha as a historical figure; Basavanna's Vachanas are preserved in dated Kannada manuscripts; Guru Nanak's compositions are in the Adi Granth. Age-band B/C.

#### Candidate Episode Titles

1. **"The Wandering Teacher: Gautama Buddha at Bodh Gaya"** — The historical Siddhartha Gautama as documented via Ashokan edicts (which name him), Pali texts, and archaeology (Bodh Gaya, Sarnath, Kapilavastu); separates the ascetic life-choice and First Sermon from later miraculous accretions.
2. **"Mahavira's Last Wandering"** — Vardhamana Mahavira as a historical reformer of the Jain ascetic tradition; his core teaching (ahimsa, non-attachment) grounded in the Ardhamagadhi *Agamas* and the attested biographies in Jain canon.
3. **"Basavanna Writes in the Street"** — The 12th-century Karnataka Lingayat movement: Basavanna's Vachanas (freely accessible composed couplets in Kannada), the Anubhava Mantapa (hall of experience), and social equality teaching; MSS tradition + inscription corroboration.
4. **"Kabir's Weaver's Riddle"** — Kabir (c. 1440-1518 CE) as a historical weaver-poet of Varanasi whose verses are preserved in three distinct traditions (Bijak, Kabir Granthavali, Adi Granth); the episode is structured around one widely-attested doha and its meaning.
5. **"Guru Nanak at the River Bein"** — The transformation episode at Sultanpur Lodhi (c. 1499 CE), grounded in Bhai Gurdas's *Vaaran* (closest near-contemporary source) and the Adi Granth compositions; separates documented compositional output from later Janamsakhi embellishments.
6. **"Andal Sings for the Temple: Kodai of Srivilliputhur"** — The 9th-century Tamil poet-saint whose *Tiruppavai* and *Nacchiyar Tirumozhi* are preserved in Divya Prabandham; early inscriptional evidence of her recognition.
7. **"Adi Shankara's Great Debate"** — Shankara (c. 788-820 CE) as a philosopher who walked from Kerala to Kashmir debating the Mimamsaka, Samkhya, and Buddhist schools; episode focuses on his documented tours and the four Mathas he established (each in a different region of India).
8. **"Thiruvalluvar's Two Thousand and Thirty-Three Couplets"** — The *Tirukkural* as a historical text that crosses caste, region, and religion; how it was compiled and transmitted; its place in Tamil classical literature alongside Sangam poetry.

#### Sensitivity Notes
- **Most sensitive vein on this list.** Every figure here belongs to a living tradition. Episodes must present the *historical-biographical* layer while scrupulously not commenting on doctrine, theology, or the validity of any religious belief.
- **Shankara:** The traditional dates (788-820 CE or 509-477 BCE in alternative calculations) are not settled by epigraphy. Use the range "about 1,200 years ago" rather than an exact year for children.
- **Buddha/Mahavira:** Both are historical figures confirmed by epigraphy (Buddha more so, via Ashoka). The Pali Canon and Jain Agamas are the primary literary sources, compiled centuries later — state this.
- **Kabir's community:** He is claimed by Hindu, Muslim, and Sikh traditions. Present him as belonging to all and none; his own verses refuse sectarian labelling — that *is* the story.
- **Guru Nanak:** Given Sikh community sensitivity about depiction, the episode should focus on his documented compositions in the Adi Granth rather than any visual characterisation. Age-band B/C.
- **Meerabai** (not in episode list above but mentioned in brief): Her historical dates are debated; the *Mirabai ki Padavali* compilation is post-15th century; less well-sourced than others above. Recommend as a later-phase episode with careful dating.

### 6 — MARITIME & TRADE HISTORY *(Good Priority — extends proven Chola/Rajendra thread)*

**Rationale:** India sat at the centre of the Indian Ocean world for 2,000 years. This vein turns the existing Chola maritime hint (Rajendra episode) into a full thematic thread, bringing in the Tamil merchant guilds, the Malabar spice trade, Lothal's dock, and India's diplomatic footprint in Southeast Asia. Visually spectacular (dhow ships, spice bazaars, coral harbours); demonstrates India's role as a *giving* civilisation, not only a defending one.

#### Candidate Episode Titles

1. **"The Pepper Boats of Muziris"** — The Roman-era spice port of Muziris (Kodungallur, Kerala), documented in the *Periplus Maris Erythraei* (~1st c. CE) as a city of Roman amphorae, Indian pepper, and multilingual merchants.
2. **"The Five Hundred Guild Sails South"** — The Ayyavole merchant guild of Karnataka (9th-13th c.): their inscriptions in Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, and Sri Lanka documenting pan-regional trade networks and civic philanthropy.
3. **"Chola Ships in the Srivijaya Straits"** — Rajendra Chola I's 1025 CE naval expedition to Srivijaya (Sumatra), documented in the Tirumalai inscription; the episode explores *why* empires sail rather than only *how*.
4. **"Zheng He Finds the Spice Coast"** — The Chinese admiral's 1405-1433 voyages to Calicut (Kozhikode) and Cochin; described in Ma Huan's *Yingya Shenglan* (1433) and confirmed by Indian Ocean archaeological finds.
5. **"The Borobudur Ship Visits Nagapattinam"** — The famous ship carving on Borobudur's walls (c. 800 CE, Java) as evidence of Indian Ocean maritime connection; the story of how ideas and faiths travelled on merchant ships.
6. **"The Lothal Ship That Crossed to Dilmun"** — The Harappan dockyard at Lothal and the evidence of long-distance trade with Dilmun (Bahrain) and Mesopotamia via carnelian beads and standardised weights.

#### Sensitivity Notes
- **Zheng He:** A celebrated figure in Chinese national history; present as a historical visitor to India, focusing on what the Indian coastal cities showed him, without geopolitical commentary.
- **Chola naval expedition:** Frame as cultural and trade diplomacy as much as military; inscriptions support tribute/political acknowledgement, not colonisation.
- **Lothal-Mesopotamia trade:** Ground in archaeological evidence (bead analysis, weight standards); avoid speculative claims about cultural transfer.

### 7 — REGIONAL KINGDOMS OFTEN SKIPPED *(Medium Priority — fills geographic equity gaps)*

**Rationale:** The existing 19 Ancient/Medieval episodes are heavily weighted toward north India (Mauryas, Guptas) and peninsular powers (Cholas, Pallavas, Vijayanagara). Several equally well-documented kingdoms — the Kakatiyas of Andhra, the Hoysalas of Karnataka, the Sangam-era Cheras and Pandyas, the Sikh Empire — remain entirely absent. This vein corrects geographic inequity without needing a new "section" — episodes slot into the existing Ancient & Medieval pillar or a new "Kingdoms of India" sub-section.

#### Candidate Episode Titles

1. **"The Thousand-Pillar Temple of Warangal"** — Kakatiya kingdom (Andhra, 12th-14th c.): the Ramappa Temple (UNESCO World Heritage 2021) and its floating brick innovations; builder records in Kakatiya inscriptions.
2. **"Sangam Poets at the Pandya Academy"** — The Tamil Sangam literary academies (~3rd c. BCE to 3rd c. CE) as documented in the *Purananuru* and *Akananuru*; India's oldest known secular literary corpus.
3. **"The Pearl Divers of Korkai"** — Pandya pearl fishery at Korkai (southern Tamil Nadu), documented in the *Periplus* and Sangam poetry; how a luxury trade shaped a kingdom.
4. **"Hoysala Sculptors: A Hundred Figures on One Beam"** — Hoysala art at Belur and Halebidu (12th-14th c.): the star-shaped temple plan; named sculptors in inscriptions; the *soapstone* that allows such fine carving.
5. **"Ranjit Singh Opens the Lahore Durbar"** — The Sikh Empire (r. 1799-1839): Ranjit Singh's court as documented by the court chronicler Sohan Lal Suri (*Umdat-ut-Tawarikh*), the treaty with the British East India Company, and the Koh-i-Noor.
6. **"The Zamorin Sends a Letter to Calicut's Sea"** — The Samudri Raja (Zamorin) of Kozhikode and Malabar's spice trade politics; documented in Duarte Barbosa (1516), the Keralolpatti, and Ibn Battuta.
7. **"Marthanda Varma Builds the Navy of Travancore"** — 18th-century Travancore king who built a standing naval force and the Valiamala wall; documented in Travancore State Manual and Dutch VOC records.

- **Ramappa Temple (Kakatiya):** The claim of "floating bricks" (lightweight pumice-like bricks that float) is documented in modern engineering analysis and ASI reports — verified and child-friendly.

### 8 — EXPLORERS, CHRONICLERS & CULTURAL EXCHANGE *(Medium Priority — deepens "outside eyes" thread)*

**Rationale:** The existing Xuanzang episode demonstrates that the "foreign visitor's amazed gaze" is a powerful narrative device for children. This vein extends it across 1,500 years: Faxian (5th c.), Al-Biruni (11th c.), Ibn Battuta (14th c.), and Ashoka's own ambassadors to Greek kingdoms. Each traveller left a primary text of exceptional quality that doubles as a primary source *for Indian history* — making them both subjects and sources. The thread also establishes India as a place other civilisations *came to learn from*, which is a celebratory framing.

#### Candidate Episode Titles

1. **"Faxian Walks from China to the Ganges"** — Faxian's overland journey to India (~399-414 CE), his residence at Pataliputra, and his description of the Gupta golden age; primary: *Foguo Ji* (Record of Buddhist Kingdoms). Companion to the existing Chandragupta II episode.
2. **"Al-Biruni Learns Sanskrit to Understand India"** — Abu Rayhān al-Biruni (~1017-1030 CE) mastering Sanskrit and writing *Kitab-ul-Hind*, the most systematic foreign analysis of Indian science, philosophy, and society before the modern era.
3. **"Ibn Battuta at the Delhi Minarets"** — Ibn Battuta at Muhammad bin Tughluq's court (~1334-1341): his *Rihla* documents Delhi's size, the post-horse relay system, the horrifying and brilliant aspects of Tughluq's reign, and a harrowing journey to the coast.
4. **"The Ambassadors Ashoka Sent West"** — Rock Edict XIII names five Hellenistic kings (Antiochus, Ptolemy, Antigonus, Magas, Alexander) to whom Ashoka sent dhamma ambassadors; the episode explores what it meant for a Mauryan empire to reach diplomatically to the Mediterranean.
5. **"Marco Polo Sees the Ships of Malabar"** — Marco Polo's description of Indian Ocean trade, Indian ships, and south Indian kingdoms (~1292-93); useful as a counterpoint to Polo's astonishment — India shows him marvels, not the other way around.

#### Sensitivity Notes
- **Ibn Battuta / Tughluq's Delhi:** Muhammad bin Tughluq's reign is controversial (mass punishments, forced relocations). The episode should focus on what Ibn Battuta *observed about India's administrative and commercial infrastructure*, not on the sultan's harshness.
- **Al-Biruni:** His work was commissioned during Mahmud of Ghazni's invasions of India — a sensitive historical context. Frame the episode on *Biruni's intellectual humility and genuine scholarship* rather than the political context. His Sanskrit learning and his honest acknowledgement of Indian intellectual superiority in astronomy/mathematics is the story.
- **Ashoka's ambassadors:** The Hellenistic kings named are confirmed by cross-referencing with Greek historical sources. This is one of the finest examples of Indian epigraphy as international diplomatic history.

## Key Gaps & Uncertainties in This Briefing

1. **Kailasa Temple builder attribution:** While most scholars attribute the Kailasa Temple (Ellora) to Rashtrakuta king Krishna I (c. 756-773 CE) based on the Baroda copper plates, a dedicated researcher should verify against the most current ASI/Deccan College work, as some scholars have debated phasing.
2. **Rudrama Devi coinage:** The researcher should verify via Numismatic Society of India publications that the coins bearing "Sri Rudradeva" titulature are specifically attributed to Rudramadevi rather than an earlier Kakatiya male king of similar name before writing the episode.
3. **Madhava dates:** Madhava of Sangamagrama's dates are given as c. 1340-1425 CE in most sources; K.V. Sarma gives c. 1350 CE as his birth. The episode should use "about 700 years ago" for children rather than a precise year.
4. **Meerabai** (mentioned in the original brief but not developed above): Her historical documentation is considerably thinner than the others in Vein 5. Charlotte Vaudeville's scholarship and the earliest manuscript collections of her *padavali* would need careful review before a sourced episode can be written. Recommended as Phase 3.
5. **Naganika's Nanaghat inscription reading:** Georg Bühler's 1883 reading of the Nanaghat inscription has been debated in subsequent scholarship (some readings differ on the queen's name and relationship to the king). A researcher should use D.C. Sircar's *Select Inscriptions* Vol. I edition as the current standard before writing the episode.
6. **Sangam literature dating:** The consensus date-range (c. 300 BCE to 300 CE) for the classical Sangam corpus is widely accepted but the earlier extreme (Tolkappiyam) has scholars arguing for dates up to 500 BCE. The episodes should use a range ("ancient Tamil literature, roughly 2,000 years old") rather than a fixed date.
